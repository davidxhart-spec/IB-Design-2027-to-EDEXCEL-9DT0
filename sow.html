NEA Brief Guidance for Online Students

The most significant challenge for online A level D&T delivery is the physical prototyping requirement of Component 2. The guidance below helps students choose a design context that is genuinely achievable without sustained workshop access, while still meeting the full assessment criteria.

Brief Selection Principles

  • The prototype must be physically made and photographed — digital-only is not sufficient.
  • Aim for briefs solvable with hand tools, a domestic sewing machine, basic electronics, or occasional makerspace visits.
  • The portfolio (design process evidence) can be entirely digital — this is an advantage for online students.
  • Steer students toward a real client or user group they can access for research and feedback.
  • The iterative design process must be evident — choose a brief complex enough to generate genuine design decisions.

✔ Ideal for online students

BriefWhy it works
Flat-pack / knock-down furnitureSimple geometry, wood/ply, hand tools or CNC at makerspace. Easily photographed and tested.
Packaging designCard, paper, nets. Foldable prototypes at home. Strong sustainability angle for extended Q links.
Electronic product (enclosure + circuit)Arduino or similar. Circuit on Tinkercad; enclosure in laser-cut ply or 3D print at library/makerspace.
Textiles / wearable accessoryHand-sewn or domestic sewing machine. Easily sourced materials. Good ergonomics angle.
Desktop organiser / stationery productSmall scale. Acrylic/ply/card. Laser cutter output or hand-made. Clear user group.
Assistive / adaptive deviceUser-centred design focus. Low-complexity prototype. Strong evaluation criteria.

⚠ Manageable with planning

BriefConsideration
Small seating / furnitureNeeds workshop access. Plan makerspace visits early. Flat-pack approach reduces tool dependency.
Kitchen / cooking productFood-safe material choices add complexity. Prototype can be scaled down or modelled digitally first.
Children's toySafety standards (BS EN 71) must be addressed. Physical prototype needed but small scale manageable.
Lighting productElectrical safety considerations. LED circuits straightforward. Shade/body can be laser-cut or formed.

✗ Avoid for online delivery

BriefWhy to avoid
Large furniture (chairs, tables)Requires sustained workshop access, large stock materials, specialist tools. Impractical at home.
Metal fabrication projectsWelding, casting, forming — cannot be replicated at home.
Complex mechanismsPrecision engineering tolerances. Specialist equipment required. High risk of incomplete prototype.
Injection-moulded productsCommercial process — cannot prototype authentically without industrial access.

Adapting Component 2 for Online Delivery

AreaOnline Approach
PrototypingStudents source materials locally (Hobbycraft, B&Q, Amazon). Agree a materials budget upfront. Provide a recommended materials list per brief category. Makerspace.org.uk directory helps locate local laser/CNC/3D print access.
Portfolio reviewFortnightly screen-share sessions (15–20 min per student). Student shares live portfolio PDF or Notability/GoodNotes file. Teacher annotates live using iPad + Apple Pencil. Record sessions for student reference.
Making evidenceStudents photograph and short-video every making stage. Upload weekly to shared folder. Teacher responds with written feedback asynchronously. This becomes portfolio evidence.
CAB annotationComplete CAB digitally alongside student. Use Edexcel's fillable PDF. Screen-share to walk through grid criteria together before final submission.
Client / user researchOnline surveys (Google Forms, Typeform). Video interviews with real users. Screenshot evidence counts as primary research.
TestingSystematic testing against specification points. Students film product being tested/used. Annotated photos of test results. Quantitative where possible (dimensions, weight, load).
SubmissionAll digital via Learner Work Transfer. Portfolio as PDF, prototype photos as JPEG, video evidence optional. Deadline: 15 May. Submit centre marks first, then upload sample.

Recommended Student Toolkit

  • Autodesk Fusion 360 (free education licence) — CAD modelling, rendering, technical drawing
  • Tinkercad — quick 3D modelling and circuit simulation
  • Gravity Sketch (iPad) — ideation sketching with Apple Pencil
  • Canva or Adobe Express — portfolio layout
  • Google Forms — user surveys and client feedback
  • Makerspace.org.uk — locate nearby laser/CNC/3D print facilities
